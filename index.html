A Data Science Approach to Population Balanced Redistricting

John Aidan Ziolo - Independent Research Project

Project Summary

Gerrymandering happens when politicians draw congressional districts in a way that gives their political party an unfair advantage, often devaluing a citizen’s vote or even turning it against their intended representation. In this project, I developed a possible solution using data science and AI techniques, specifically K-Means Clustering and Gradient Descent. The goal was to create congressional districts automatically, without factoring in people’s votes, beliefs, race, or income—only focusing on population balance and geometric compactness.

To test the idea, I first created a small model state called “Coloroming,” with simulated cities and rural areas. K-Means Clustering was used to place initial district centers by clustering population points, which produced visually clean districts but large population imbalances. To fix this, Gradient Descent was added to fine-tune the districts by adjusting the position and “weight” of each district so population could be shared more evenly. After training, the districts in Coloroming were nearly perfectly balanced while maintaining simple shapes.

After that, the model was applied to real states, including Georgia, Louisiana, and Illinois. In each case, the generated districts were closer in population than the real congressional maps and stayed well within legal population deviation limits. While the model does not account for factors like county lines, geography, or minority representation, it shows that unbiased, math-driven districting is possible. With better real-world data and further improvements, this system could help make elections fairer for everyone.
